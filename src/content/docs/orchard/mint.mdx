The Mint view is where you operate the Cashu mint at the center of your stack: the public
face wallets see, the keys it issues against, how it behaves, and the database that holds
its state. It's organised into five tabs — **Dashboard**, **Info**, **Config**, **Keysets**,
and **Database**.

## Dashboard

The mint's overview, read-only: cards for its info, supported nuts and limits, keysets, and
an **Activity** panel tracking operations and volume — mints, melts, and swaps over a day,
week, month, or year — plus a **Balance Sheet**. Below them, deeper "Nutalytics" charts plot
balances, mints, melts, swaps, fee revenue, and ecash counts across a date range you choose.

## Info

The public metadata wallets read when they connect — edited here rather than in the mint's
own config:

- **Name**, **description**, and a longer **description**.
- **Icon** — the image a wallet displays for your mint.
- **Connection URLs** — where wallets reach the mint.
- **Contacts** — how users get in touch (email, X, Nostr, and so on).
- **Message of the Day** — a short broadcast note.

Keep it accurate: this is what a wallet shows someone before they trust your mint.

## Config

How the mint behaves:

- **Minting (NUT-04)** and **Melting (NUT-05)** — switch each on or off, set the per-unit
  minimum and maximum amounts for BOLT11 and BOLT12, and the quote expiry (TTL).
- **Supported nuts** — a reference list of the rest of the protocol features your mint
  supports (token state checks, fee return, signature restore, P2PK, HTLCs, and more); some
  are toggled here, others are set in the mint's own config file.

## Keysets

The keys your mint issues ecash against. The table lists every keyset — its ID, unit,
active state, fee rate (ppk), balance, fee revenue, and proof and promise counts — with a
chart of activity over time. Run a **keyset rotation** to start issuing against fresh keys,
choosing the unit, input fee, and amounts.

## Database

Back up and restore the mint database straight from the dashboard, so keeping a current copy
doesn't mean dropping to the command line:

- **Create Backup** downloads a copy of the database.
- **Restore Backup** loads one back in — shut the mint down first, match the backup to the
  mint's version, restore, then start it again.

The same screen also browses the underlying mint, melt, and swap records, filterable by
date, unit, and state.

### The recovery model

A mint is restored from two things together:

- **The seed phrase**, which derives the mint's signing keys. It never changes, lives in the
  mint daemon's config rather than its database, and Orchard never holds it — so this half
  stays your responsibility (see the mint's [seed step](/new-mint/mint/#generate-the-mint-seed)).
- **The database**, which changes every time the mint issues or redeems ecash — the half
  Orchard backs up for you.

With both, a mint lost to a dead disk comes back fully operational, with the same keysets and
the same outstanding ecash. With only one, it doesn't. Take backups on a schedule and store
them off the machine, separate from your seed backup.
